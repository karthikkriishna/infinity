  • From Jan 21Series

    Number Theory Seminar Series

    A tentative series of 14 talks on number theory, delivered by ex-Convenor Manu A Sankaran in the AB2 Seminar Room.

    Read abstract

    Historically, number theory has its origins in real-world problems — the measurement of right angles using integral lengths predates even Pythagoras. A natural generalization of these questions is the study of Diophantine equations: polynomial equations in several indeterminates for which solutions are sought in the integers, or more loosely, the rationals.

    This historical development usually leads to algebraic number theory, where studying certain field extensions helps resolve such problems — for instance, the equation x³ + 1 = y² has finitely many solutions, a fact established via factorisation in a cyclotomic extension of the integers. The same foundations can address Diophantine equations in a different context: understanding their solutions modulo certain integers. The Chinese Remainder Theorem reduces this to prime power moduli, which in turn leads to an alternate number system with several non-standard but interesting properties.

    This series addresses these systems, their properties, and their applications to problems in algebraic number theory.
